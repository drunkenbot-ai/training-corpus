# The Great Book of Life on Earth  

## Chapter 1: The Beginning  

Life started small. Very small.  
It began in water. Warm water.  
The first life was a single cell.  
It had no eyes. No mouth. No brain.  
It just floated and copied itself.  
That was over 3 billion years ago.  
The earth was hot. The air had no oxygen.  
But life found a way.  

## Chapter 2: Bacteria  

Bacteria are tiny. You cannot see them.  
They are everywhere. In soil. In water. In your body.  
They are simple cells. No nucleus.  
They divide every 20 minutes. Fast.  
Some bacteria make you sick.  
Most bacteria are helpful.  
They break down dead things.  
They turn milk into yogurt.  
They help you digest food.  
Without bacteria, life would stop.  
Bacteria were the first life forms.  
They changed the earth.  
They made oxygen.  
They made the air breathable.  
They are tough. They live in hot springs.  
They live in ice. They live in acid.  
They live in your stomach.  
There are more bacteria on earth than stars.  
Billions of billions.  
They are ancient. They are wise.  
They have been here longest.  

## Chapter 3: Viruses  

Viruses are not quite alive.  
They are smaller than bacteria. Much smaller.  
They are just a bit of genetic code.  
Wrapped in a protein coat.  
They cannot do anything alone.  
They need a host.  
They sneak into cells.  
They take over the cell's machinery.  
They make copies of themselves.  
Then they burst out.  
The cell dies. The virus spreads.  
Viruses cause colds. And flu. And worse.  
But viruses also help.  
They keep bacteria in check.  
They move genes between species.  
They drive evolution.  
Viruses are ancient. Older than bacteria.  
They have been infecting cells forever.  
They are tiny. They are powerful.  
They shape life on earth.  

## Chapter 4: Archaea  

Archaea look like bacteria.  
But they are different. Very different.  
Their cell walls are unique.  
Their genes are unique.  
They live in extreme places.  
Boiling hot springs. Deep ocean vents.  
Salty lakes. Acid pools.  
They love methane. They make methane.  
They live without oxygen.  
They are the extremophiles.  
They are ancient. Very ancient.  
They are our distant cousins.  
We share a common ancestor.  
They taught life how to survive.  
They are tough. They are resilient.  
They are everywhere. In the soil. In the sea.  
In your gut. They are quiet. They are strong.  
They are the unsung heroes of life.  

## Chapter 5: Protists  

Protists are single-celled. But complex.  
They have a nucleus. They have organelles.  
They are like tiny factories.  
Some are like animals. They move. They eat.  
Some are like plants. They photosynthesize.  
Some are both. They are mixotrophs.  
Amoebas are protists. They ooze along.  
They eat bacteria. They change shape.  
Paramecia are protists. They swim with hair.  
They have two nuclei. One for housekeeping.  
One for reproduction. Smart.  
Diatoms are protists. They have glass shells.  
Beautiful. Delicate. They float in the sea.  
They make oxygen. Lots of it.  
Dinoflagellates are protists. They glow at night.  
They cause red tides. They poison fish.  
Protists are the base of the food web.  
Without them, no fish. No whales. No us.  
They are tiny. They are mighty.  
They have been here for a billion years.  

## Chapter 6: Fungi  

Fungi are not plants. Not animals.  
They are a kingdom of their own.  
They are made of threads. Called hyphae.  
The threads form a web. Called mycelium.  
Most fungi you cannot see.  
They live underground. Or inside wood.  
The mushroom is just the fruit.  
Like an apple on a tree.  
Fungi eat dead things. They break them down.  
They recycle nutrients. They are the garbage crew.  
Some fungi live with plants. In the roots.  
They give water and minerals.  
Plants give sugar. It is a deal.  
This is mycorrhiza. It is ancient.  
Without fungi, no forests. No trees.  
Fungi are also medicines. Penicillin comes from mold.  
Some fungi are poisonous. Some are delicious.  
Yeast is a fungus. It makes bread rise.  
It makes beer. It makes wine.  
Fungi are everywhere. In the air. In the soil.  
On your skin. Between your toes.  
They are patient. They are persistent.  
They are the hidden web of life.  

## Chapter 7: Algae  

Algae are like plants. But simpler.  
They live in water. Mostly.  
They are green. Or red. Or brown.  
They photosynthesize. They make oxygen.  
They are the lungs of the ocean.  
Seaweed is algae. Giant kelp.  
It forms underwater forests.  
Fish hide in kelp. Sea otters live there.  
Phytoplankton are algae. Microscopic.  
They float near the surface.  
They absorb sunlight. They grow.  
They are eaten by tiny animals.  
The food web starts with algae.  
Without algae, the ocean would be dead.  
Algae also make oil. Ancient algae.  
That oil became petroleum.  
We burn it. We drive cars.  
Algae are also food. Nori. Spirulina.  
They are nutritious. They are sustainable.  
Algae are old. Older than plants.  
They gave plants their chloroplasts.  
They are the green ancestors of all leaves.  

## Chapter 8: Mosses and Liverworts  

Mosses are small plants. Very small.  
They have no roots. No real stems.  
They just cling to rocks and soil.  
They love damp places. Forests. Bogs.  
They reproduce with spores. Tiny dust.  
They need water for sex. The sperm swims.  
Mosses are ancient. They were the first land plants.  
They crawled out of water 400 million years ago.  
They made soil. They broke down rocks.  
They paved the way for bigger plants.  
Liverworts are like mosses. Flatter. Greasier.  
They also live in damp places.  
They are simple. They are humble.  
They cover the forest floor.  
They hold moisture. They keep the ground cool.  
They are home to tiny bugs.  
Mosses are tough. They can dry out.  
Then they revive with water. Magic.  
They survive in the Arctic. In deserts.  
They are the pioneers of plant life.  

## Chapter 9: Ferns  

Ferns are ancient plants. Older than dinosaurs.  
They have real roots. Real stems.  
They have leaves. Called fronds.  
They unroll as they grow. Like a fiddlehead.  
Ferns reproduce with spores. Not seeds.  
Spores are on the underside of leaves.  
Tiny brown dots. Sori.  
Ferns love shade. And moisture.  
They grow in forests. Near streams.  
They can be tall. Tree ferns.  
They can be tiny. Filmy ferns.  
They were the first big plants.  
They formed the first forests.  
They changed the climate.  
They pulled carbon from the air.  
They made coal. Ancient fern forests.  
We burn that coal today.  
Ferns are survivors. They outlasted dinosaurs.  
They are everywhere. Except the desert.  
They are beautiful. They are delicate.  
They are a living fossil.  

## Chapter 10: Conifers  

Conifers are trees. They have cones.  
They have needles instead of leaves.  
They are evergreen. They stay green all year.  
They include pines. Firs. Spruces. Cedars.  
They grow in cold places. Mountains. Taiga.  
They are tall. Straight. Strong.  
They make wood. Used for houses. Paper.  
They reproduce with seeds. In cones.  
The seeds have no fruit. Just a wing.  
The wind carries them.  
Conifers are ancient. Older than flowers.  
They were the dominant trees for millions of years.  
They are tough. They survive winter.  
Their needles have anti-freeze.  
They are adapted to fire. Some need fire.  
Heat opens their cones. Seeds fall.  
They are the forests of the north.  
They are home to moose. Owls. Squirrels.  
They are quiet. They are majestic.  
They are the backbone of boreal life.  

## Chapter 11: Flowering Plants  

Flowering plants are angiosperms.  
They are the most successful plants.  
They have flowers. Fruits. Seeds.  
They appeared 140 million years ago.  
They changed the world.  
Flowers attract insects. Bees. Butterflies.  
Pollination. The insects carry pollen.  
Fruits protect seeds. Animals eat fruits.  
Seeds pass through guts. Dispersal.  
Angiosperms are everywhere. Grass. Trees. Herbs.  
They include roses. Oaks. Wheat. Rice.  
They feed the world. We eat their grains.  
Their fruits. Their vegetables.  
They give us medicine. Aspirin from willow.  
Quinine from cinchona. Morphine from poppy.  
They are beautiful. They are diverse.  
Over 300,000 species.  
They dominate every habitat.  
Rainforests. Deserts. Tundra. Oceans.  
They are the green carpet of the earth.  

## Chapter 12: Grasses  

Grasses are flowering plants. But simple.  
They have narrow leaves. Hollow stems.  
They grow from the base. Not the tip.  
You can mow them. They keep growing.  
Grasses include wheat. Rice. Corn.  
They are the staple food of humanity.  
They are the savannah. The prairie.  
The pampas. The steppe.  
They feed grazing animals. Cows. Bison. Zebras.  
They are tough. They survive fire.  
They survive drought. They survive grazing.  
They spread by runners. They form turf.  
Bamboo is a grass. It grows fast.  
Three feet in a day. Amazing.  
It is used for building. For food.  
Grasses are wind-pollinated. No flowers needed.  
They release pollen in clouds. It causes hay fever.  
But they also give us sugar. Sugarcane is grass.  
They give us beer. Barley is grass.  
They are the base of the food chain.  
On land. Everywhere.  
Grasses are humble. They are essential.  
They are the breadbasket of the world.  

## Chapter 13: Trees  

Trees are large plants. Woody. Tall.  
They have a trunk. Branches. Leaves.  
They are the lungs of the earth.  
They absorb carbon dioxide. Release oxygen.  
They provide shade. Shelter. Homes.  
Birds nest in them. Squirrels live in them.  
Trees are ancient. Some live thousands of years.  
Bristlecone pines. Oldest living things.  
Trees communicate. Through roots. Through fungi.  
They warn each other of insects.  
They share nutrients. They are social.  
Deciduous trees lose leaves in winter.  
Oaks. Maples. Elms.  
Evergreen trees keep leaves. Pines. Firs.  
Tropical trees have broad leaves. Rainforests.  
They are tall. Emergent layer.  
Trees prevent erosion. Roots hold soil.  
They make rain. They release water vapor.  
They are the climate regulators.  
Without trees, earth would be a desert.  
We cut them down. Too fast.  
We need to plant more.  
Trees are life. They are sacred.  
They are the pillars of the biosphere.  

## Chapter 14: Rainforests  

Rainforests are dense. Wet. Green.  
They are near the equator. Hot all year.  
They get 100 inches of rain annually.  
They are the most diverse places.  
Half of all species live there.  
Insects. Birds. Monkeys. Frogs.  
Trees are tall. Canopy is thick.  
Sunlight barely reaches the ground.  
The floor is dark. Damp.  
Fungi grow everywhere. Decay is fast.  
Nutrients are in the plants. Not the soil.  
The soil is thin. Poor.  
But the trees are massive.  
Epiphytes grow on trees. Orchids. Bromeliads.  
They get water from air. Nutrients from debris.  
Vines climb to the canopy. Lianas.  
They are thick as ropes.  
Rainforests produce oxygen. Lots of it.  
They are the world's pharmacy.  
Many medicines come from here.  
Curare. Quinine. Vincristine.  
But we are losing them.  
Burning. Logging. Farming.  
Every second, a football field disappears.  
We must save them. They are irreplaceable.  
They are the jewel of the earth.  

## Chapter 15: Oceans  

Oceans cover 70% of earth.  
They are deep. Vast. Mysterious.  
They are home to most life.  
Plankton. Fish. Whales. Worms.  
The surface is bright. Full of sunlight.  
Phytoplankton grow there. They make oxygen.  
Half of all oxygen comes from oceans.  
The deep sea is dark. Cold. Pressurized.  
Yet life thrives there.  
Vent communities. Around hydrothermal vents.  
They use chemicals. Not sunlight.  
Chemosynthesis. Bacteria do it.  
They make food from sulfur.  
Tube worms live there. Red. Long.  
They have no mouth. No gut.  
Bacteria live inside them. They feed each other.  
Deep sea fish are weird. Anglerfish. Lanternfish.  
They have lights. Bioluminescence.  
They attract prey. Or mates.  
Oceans have currents. They move heat.  
They regulate climate. They absorb carbon.  
Coral reefs are the rainforests of the sea.  
Colorful. Diverse. Fragile.  
They are dying. From warming. Acidification.  
We must protect the oceans.  
They are the blue heart of the planet.  

## Chapter 16: Coral Reefs  

Corals are animals. Tiny. Colonial.  
They build calcium carbonate skeletons.  
They form reefs. Large. Massive.  
Reefs are underwater cities.  
Fish. Crabs. Shrimp. Snails.  
They all live together.  
Corals have algae inside. Zooxanthellae.  
The algae photosynthesize. Feed the coral.  
The coral gives shelter. It is mutualism.  
Reefs are colorful. Parrotfish. Clownfish.  
Butterflyfish. Damselfish.  
They are the most diverse marine habitats.  
Only 1% of ocean area.  
But 25% of marine species.  
Reefs protect coastlines. From waves. Storms.  
They are nurseries for fish.  
They support fisheries. Feed millions.  
But reefs are bleaching.  
When water gets hot, algae leave.  
Coral turns white. Dies.  
Pollution also kills reefs.  
Overfishing. Dynamite. Cyanide.  
We must stop it.  
Reefs are treasures. They are dying.  
We must act now.  

## Chapter 17: Insects  

Insects are arthropods. Six legs. Exoskeleton.  
They are the most diverse animals.  
Over a million species. Probably more.  
They are everywhere. Land. Freshwater. Air.  
They have wings. Most do.  
They were the first to fly.  
Insects include beetles. Butterflies. Ants. Bees.  
They are pollinators. They make fruit. They make seeds.  
Without insects, no flowers. No crops.  
They are decomposers. Dung beetles. Carrion beetles.  
They clean up waste. They recycle.  
They are food. For birds. Lizards. Frogs.  
Ants are social. They live in colonies.  
Queens. Workers. Soldiers.  
They farm fungi. They herd aphids.  
They build huge nests. Underground.  
Termites are social. They eat wood.  
They have gut bacteria. They digest cellulose.  
They are architects. Their mounds are tall.  
Bees make honey. They are vital.  
They pollinate everything.  
But bees are dying. Pesticides. Disease.  
We need them. We must save them.  
Insects are small. They are mighty.  
They are the little things that run the world.  

## Chapter 18: Spiders and Arachnids  

Spiders are not insects. Eight legs.  
They have two body parts. Cephalothorax. Abdomen.  
They make silk. Strong. Flexible.  
They spin webs. To catch prey.  
Orb weavers. Pretty webs. Symmetrical.  
They wait in the center. Patience.  
Jumping spiders. They pounce. Active hunters.  
They have good eyes. Eight of them.  
Wolf spiders. They chase. No web.  
Tarantulas are huge. Hairy. Harmless.  
They eat insects. And small vertebrates.  
Scorpions have pincers. And a stinger.  
They are nocturnal. They glow under UV light.  
Mites and ticks are arachnids.  
Ticks spread disease. Lyme disease.  
Mites live on skin. In hair follicles.  
Spiders are predators. They control insects.  
Without them, we would be overrun.  
They are ancient. Fossil spiders.  
300 million years old.  
They are fascinating. They are scary.  
But they are our allies.

Crustaceans are arthropods. Water-dwellers.  
They have shells. Two pairs of antennae.  
They include crabs. Lobsters. Shrimp.  
Krill are tiny crustaceans.  
They are food for whales.  
Copepods are smaller. Plankton.  
They are the most abundant animal.  
In the ocean. Everywhere.  
Crabs are scavengers. They eat dead things.

Hermit crabs use snail shells. For protection.

They include snails. Clams. Squid. Octopus.  
Snails are gastropods. One shell.  
They glide on slime. A muscular foot.  
They are slow. They eat plants. Or meat.  
Clams are bivalves. Two shells.

Oysters make pearls. From irritation.

Mollusks are diverse. 100,000 species.

They are important. In the food web.

## Chapter 21: Worms  

Worms are long. Soft. Legless.  
They include earthworms. Roundworms. Flatworms.  
Earthworms are annelids. Segmented.  
They live in soil. They eat dirt.  
They excrete castings. Rich fertilizer.  
They aerate soil. Roots grow better.  
Earthworms are the gardeners of the earth.  
Roundworms are nematodes. Everywhere.  
In soil. In water. In animals.  
Many are parasites. Hookworms. Pinworms.  
They infect humans. And livestock.  
But most are free-living. Invisible.  
Flatworms are platyhelminthes.  
Tapeworms are flat. Parasitic.  
They live in guts. Absorb food.  
They can be long. Several feet.  
Planaria are free-living flatworms.  
They regenerate. Cut them. They grow back.  
Incredible. Science studies them.  
Worms are simple. But vital.  
They are the base of many food chains.  
They turn dead leaves into soil.  
They are humble. They are hardworking.  
Without worms, the earth would be barren.

## Chapter 30: Microbes in the Gut
