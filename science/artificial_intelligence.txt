Tokens, Tokenization, Embeddings, and Vocabulary

Language is made of words.
Computers do not understand words directly.
They process numbers.
Every piece of text must become numbers.

The first step is tokenization.
Tokenization divides text into smaller parts.
These parts are called tokens.
Tokens become the language of the model.

A token is not always a word.
Sometimes one word is one token.
Sometimes one word becomes several tokens.
Sometimes one token contains punctuation.

For example, the word "computer" may be one token.
A longer word may become several tokens.
Very common words often stay together.
Rare words may split into pieces.

Different tokenizers work differently.
Each follows specific rules.
The choice affects efficiency.
It also affects model performance.

Tokenization reduces complexity.
The model processes manageable pieces.
Learning becomes easier.
Prediction becomes faster.

Every token receives a unique number.
This number is called a token ID.
The model processes token IDs.
Words themselves are never used directly.

Suppose the token "cat" has ID 152.
The token "dog" may have ID 289.
The token "tree" may have ID 901.
These numbers are examples only.

The order of tokens matters.
Changing the order changes meaning.
Language depends on sequence.
Context is extremely important.

Consider two simple sentences.
"The dog chased the cat."
"The cat chased the dog."
The words are similar but the meanings differ.

The tokenizer preserves order.
Every token keeps its position.
Later calculations depend on it.
