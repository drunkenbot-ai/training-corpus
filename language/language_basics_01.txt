Mina opens a small book.
She sees letters on the page.
Letters make words.
Words make sentences.
A sentence tells an idea.

The word run is a verb.
A verb shows action.
Birds fly.
Fish swim.
Children play.

The word blue is an adjective.
An adjective describes a thing.
The sky is blue.
The cup is small.
The road is long.

I, you, he, she, it, we, and they are pronouns.
A pronoun can replace a name.
Ravi is reading.
He is reading.
Mina and Ravi are friends.
They are friends.

A question asks for an answer.
Where is the ball?
The ball is under the chair.
Why is the floor wet?
Water spilled on the floor.

A period ends a statement.
The cat sleeps.
A question mark ends a question.
Where is the cat?
An exclamation mark shows strong feeling.
That was loud!

Past tense can show something already happened.
I walk today.
I walked yesterday.
I cook today.
I cooked yesterday.

Future tense can show something later.
I will walk tomorrow.
She will read after lunch.
They will play after school.

Good writing is clear.
Clear writing uses simple words.
A paragraph holds related sentences.
One paragraph can explain one idea.

Read this passage.
Lena plants a seed.
She gives it water.
The seed grows into a small plant.
The plant needs light.

Who plants the seed?
Lena plants the seed.
What does the plant need?
The plant needs light.
Why does Lena give water?
Water helps the seed grow.

Rewrite this sentence.
The big dog ran fast.
The large dog ran quickly.

Summarize the passage.
Lena cares for a seed.
The seed becomes a plant.

Two words can be opposites.
Hot is the opposite of cold.
Day is the opposite of night.
Open is the opposite of closed.

Two words can mean nearly the same thing.
Small and little are similar.
Fast and quick are similar.
Begin and start are similar.

Context helps meaning.
The bat flew at night.
This bat is an animal.
The player held a bat.
This bat is a tool for a game.

Good readers ask questions.
Who is this about?
What happened?
Where did it happen?
Why did it happen?
What happened next?
